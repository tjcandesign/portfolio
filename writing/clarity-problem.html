Brand Strategy

The clarity problem most brands don't know they have

By TJ Cichecki · February 2026

Almost every brand engagement I take on starts the same way. The client tells me they need a new logo. Or a website refresh. Or updated collateral. They frame the problem as a design problem, and sometimes it is. But more often than not, what I find when I start digging is that the real issue isn't visual. It's conceptual. They don't have a design problem. They have a clarity problem.

They know what they do. They can list their services, describe their product, walk you through their process. But when you ask them to explain why any of it matters, or what makes them different from the three other companies in their space doing roughly the same thing, the conversation gets fuzzy. You get corporate language. Mission statements that could belong to anyone. Value propositions that describe a category, not a company.

That's the clarity problem. And no amount of design is going to fix it.

The symptoms show up everywhere

Once you learn to spot it, you see it constantly. A website where the homepage says three contradictory things about who the company is for. A pitch deck that buries the most compelling differentiator on slide 14. A brand that looks completely different across its social media, its sales materials, and its physical space, not because someone made a style choice, but because nobody agreed on what the brand actually is in the first place.

I worked with a professional services firm a few years back that had been through two rebrands in five years. They thought the problem was the logo both times. When I came in, I spent the first two weeks just interviewing their team. Partners, associates, operations staff, even a few clients. What I found was that nobody in the organization described the company the same way. The partners each had their own version of what the firm stood for, and that inconsistency had been filtering into every piece of communication the company put out for years.

The logo was fine. The problem was that there was no shared understanding of the story it was supposed to tell.

Why this happens

Most organizations form their identity reactively. They start out doing one thing, then they grow, add services, enter new markets, merge with another company, and at no point does anyone stop to ask whether the way they talk about themselves still reflects what they actually are. The language fossilizes. The website copy from 2018 describes a company that doesn't exist anymore, but nobody has the mandate or the time to rethink it from scratch.

Then there's the founder problem. In a lot of founder-led companies, the brand lives in the founder's head. They can articulate it perfectly in a conversation, but it's never been written down, codified, or stress-tested. So when someone else needs to represent the brand, whether that's a new hire writing an email or a marketing team building a campaign, they're working from inference. They're guessing.

Both of these situations create the same downstream effect: inconsistency. And inconsistency erodes trust. Not dramatically, not all at once, but slowly, in the way that a slightly off-pitch instrument makes a whole orchestra sound wrong even when most people can't identify which instrument is the problem.

Design can't solve what strategy hasn't defined

This is the part of brand work that I think gets overlooked, especially by organizations shopping for a designer. They see the output: the logo, the color palette, the website, the system. And they assume the work is the making of those things. In reality, the work that determines whether any of those things succeed happens before anyone opens a design file.

It's the conversation about who you are actually for. Not everyone, not the broadest possible market, but the specific people whose problem you're uniquely positioned to solve. It's the decision about what you're willing to stop saying. Every brand I work with says too many things, and part of my job is helping them figure out which three or four messages actually matter and letting go of the rest.

It's also the hard work of finding the language. Not marketing language. Not tagline language. Real language that real people inside the organization can use when they're explaining what the company does at a dinner party. If the internal team can't say it simply, it's not clear enough. And if it's not clear internally, it definitely isn't landing externally.

What clarity actually looks like

When an organization has genuine brand clarity, you can feel it. Everyone on the team describes the company roughly the same way. The website, the pitch deck, and the social presence tell the same story in different formats. New hires absorb the brand naturally because the culture and the communication are aligned. And the visual identity works not because someone picked the right font, but because it's expressing something that's actually true about the organization.

I worked with a small DC-based nonprofit that had been struggling with donor engagement for years. Their materials were professionally designed. Nothing was ugly. But nothing was landing, either. When I sat down with their executive director, she described the mission in a way that was genuinely moving. Specific, personal, urgent. Then I looked at their website, and it read like a grant application. Technically accurate, completely lifeless.

We didn't redesign anything for the first month. We just worked on language. What are you really doing, and why does it matter, and who needs to hear that? By the time we got to the visual work, the whole thing clicked into place because we finally knew what the design needed to say.

Start with the question nobody wants to answer

If you're reading this and something sounds familiar, the exercise is straightforward even if it's not easy. Get five people from your organization into a room and ask each of them to describe, in two sentences, what the company does and why it matters. Don't let them prepare. Don't give them the website copy in advance. Just ask.

If you get five different answers, you don't have a design problem. You have a clarity problem. And that's actually good news, because clarity is fixable. It just requires the willingness to sit with the uncomfortable question for a while before jumping to the comfortable answer of new visuals and a refreshed color palette.

The design will come. It always does. But it will only be as strong as the thinking underneath it.
